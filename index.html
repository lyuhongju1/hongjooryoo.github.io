As an undergraduate researcher in Physics at UC Berkeley, I am dedicated to advancing our understanding of high-energy particle experimentation and lattice field theories. My academic journey is rooted in the pursuit of knowledge across multiple disciplines, including physics, applied mathematics, philosophy, and cognitive science.

My current research focuses on computational studies within the Berkeley Nuclear Theory Group N3AS and the experimental work with the Orebi Gann Group at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. Each project aims to deepen our grasp of quantum phenomena and the fundamental principles governing the universe. I was also involved in theoretical physics, examining high energy phenomena near black holes.

In addition to my research commitments, I have actively participated in the academic community through publications and poster/conference presentations, striving to contribute meaningful insights to the field of physics and engineering.

Research

Computational LQCD Research

As part of the Berkeley Nuclear Theory Group and the Network for Neutrinos, Nuclear Astrophysics, and Symmetries (N3AS), my focus is on computational research in Lattice Quantum Chromodynamics (LQCD), investigating quantum computations of 3-body scattering amplitudes in 1+1D. This research aims to advance our understanding of quantum field theories and their implications in the physical world.

Experimental Neutrino Research

At the Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL) with the Orebi Gann Group, I contribute to experimental research involving the Eos Neutrino detector. My work includes PMT characterization, data analysis, and construction of the EOS detector, aiming to shed light on neutrino properties and behaviors.

Theory Honors Research

I also investigated firewall problems and constructed the black hole final states in terms of Dr. Nomura’s formulation of the black hole microstates (hard, soft, and far modes) at the Berkeley Center for Theoretical Physics as a part of my Physics Honors Thesis Research.
